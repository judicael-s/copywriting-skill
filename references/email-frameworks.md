# Email Copy Frameworks

Loaded by the copywriting skill when the task involves email copy — newsletters, sales emails, drip sequences, or subject lines. These frameworks are distilled from 250 newsletter emails: 92 from Eddie Shleyner (VeryGoodCopy) and 158 from Drayton Bird.

---

## Pre-Writing Hierarchy

Before drafting any email, check these in order (Drayton Bird):

1. **Targeting** — Are you writing to the right person? Wrong list kills everything.
2. **The offer** — What are you actually promising? The offer matters more than fancy creative.
3. **The copy** — Now make the offer irresistible with words.

---

## Newsletter / Educational Email Structure (Eddie Shleyner)

1. **Open with a scene or story** — never a thesis statement, never "In today's newsletter..."
2. **Let the story unfold** with minimal editorializing
3. **Pivot to a principle** via a named expert or historical example
4. **Extract the lesson** cleanly in 1-3 sentences
5. **End with a resonant short sentence** — the "click"

The entire email teaches one micro-lesson through narrative. No multi-topic roundups.

---

## Sales Email Structure (Drayton Bird)

1. **Hook** — story, provocation, or surprising fact
2. **Principle** — extracted from the story
3. **Proof** — specific data, test results, or named example
4. **Single clear CTA** — one link, one action, reduce friction
5. **Sign off** — personal, warm
6. **PS** — often the most direct ask (many readers skip to the PS)

The PS is prime real estate. Use it for the clearest, most direct version of the ask — no buildup needed.

## Subject Line Principles

**Anti-sensational wins.** Stand out by taking up less space in a crowded inbox.

- One or two words, often fragments with a colon: "Hooks:" / "Specificity:" / "Being creative:"
- No clickbait, no ALL CAPS, no emoji spam
- Curiosity through incompleteness — the subject names the topic, the email delivers the insight
- Test: would this look out of place next to a friend's email? If yes, tone it down.

---

## The 4x Draft Method (Eddie Shleyner)

For achieving brevity in any format (email, ad, landing page):

1. Brain-dump a 4x first draft (if target is 300 words, write 1,200)
2. Cut in half
3. Cut in half again
4. For each remaining sentence: "Does this say the same thing faster?" / "Is this word absolutely necessary?"

## Email-Specific Tactical Rules

**Repeat the core proposition three times.** Gallup research: ads/emails that repeat the core message three times outperform. Find slightly different ways of saying it — repetition with variation.

**One CTA per email.** Multiple links split attention. If you must include secondary links, make the primary CTA visually dominant and repeated (top + bottom or mid + PS).

**Long copy outperforms short** in direct-response email (Drayton Bird — never seen short beat long making the same proposition). This does NOT mean padding. Every sentence must earn its place. A good salesperson keeps going until the prospect buys or throws them out.

**Time pressure works when honest.** "While this is fresh on your mind" is soft urgency that respects the reader. Fake countdown timers are not.

---

## Email Copy Review Checklist

Before sending, verify:

- [ ] Opening implies a clear, strong benefit or hooks with a story
- [ ] Everything is instantly clear (funny/obscure = danger)
- [ ] Every reason to act is given; objections anticipated
- [ ] Tone is correct — no humor about serious things
- [ ] Claims are proved: testimonials, data, specifics
- [ ] Firm ask for reply — reader knows exactly what to do
- [ ] CTA is big, clear, simple
- [ ] Read aloud — sounds like someone talking
- [ ] PS contains the most direct version of the ask

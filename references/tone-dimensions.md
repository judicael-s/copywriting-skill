# Tone Dimensions Reference

Deep reference for the tone-of-voice skill. Use to audit existing voice guides, build new ones, or calibrate AI-generated copy.

---

## 1. NN/g 4 Dimensions of Tone

Source: Kate Moran, Nielsen Norman Group, 2016 — "The Four Dimensions of Tone of Voice."

NN/g identified four independent spectrums that define how a brand communicates. Each sits on a 1–7 scale — they are not binary. They are independent: a brand can be formal AND funny, or casual AND serious. Key finding: the same content rewritten in different tone profiles produced measurably different trust and credibility scores. Tone affects perception, not just aesthetics.

### The Four Dimensions

| Dimension | 1 (Left Pole) | 7 (Right Pole) | What it governs |
|---|---|---|---|
| Funny ↔ Serious | Pure comedy, no gravity | Earnest, no levity | Use of humor, wordplay, irony |
| Formal ↔ Casual | Professional register, no contractions | Colloquial, contractions, slang OK | Language register, sentence structure |
| Respectful ↔ Irreverent | Deferential, conventional | Challenges norms, provocative | Relationship to authority and convention |
| Enthusiastic ↔ Matter-of-fact | High energy, exclamation marks | Dry, understated, flat delivery | Emotional temperature of delivery |

Plot each dimension separately. Conflating them produces incoherent guidelines ("be professional but fun" with no further definition is useless).

## 2. Extended 7-Dimension Model

An operational expansion of the NN/g framework for copy briefs and audits. Each dimension runs 1–5. Use the anchor labels to set a target score per channel per brand.

### Dimension 1 — Formality

| Score | Label | Behavioral Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Very Casual | Contractions everywhere, fragments fine, slang and idioms welcome, reads like a text message |
| 2 | Casual | Contractions standard, some colloquialisms, conversational flow |
| 3 | Neutral | Mix of formal and informal, no strong register commitment |
| 4 | Formal | Full sentences preferred, sparing contractions, professional vocabulary |
| 5 | Very Formal | No contractions, passive voice acceptable, institutional register, no idioms |

### Dimension 2 — Humor

| Score | Label | Behavioral Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Always Serious | Zero jokes. Gravity is the default. Levity would feel out of place. |
| 2 | Occasionally Light | A gentle observation or mild wit at most, never forced |
| 3 | Balanced | Humor appears when it serves clarity or connection, not as a reflex |
| 4 | Wit-Forward | Regular wordplay, subverted expectations, brand personality leans comic |
| 5 | Comedy-Forward | Humor is the primary mode. The brand is a comedian first. |

### Dimension 3 — Energy

| Score | Label | Behavioral Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Reserved | Understated. Lets facts do the work. No exclamation marks. |
| 2 | Calm | Measured delivery. Confident without pushing. |
| 3 | Engaged | Active voice, present tense, moderate momentum |
| 4 | Energetic | Power words, active verbs, occasional exclamation marks, forward momentum |
| 5 | Enthusiastic | High voltage. Exclamation marks common. Every sentence moves. |

### Dimension 4 — Warmth

| Score | Label | Behavioral Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Distant | Third person, institutional, impersonal, no emotional acknowledgment |
| 2 | Neutral | Professional courtesy, not cold but not warm |
| 3 | Friendly | "You" addressed directly, occasional acknowledgment of feelings |
| 4 | Warm | We/you relationship explicit, empathetic language, emotional validation |
| 5 | Very Warm | Deep emotional mirroring, supportive language, reader feels seen |

### Dimension 5 — Authority

| Score | Label | Behavioral Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Peer-Level | Collaborative, open to uncertainty, shares learning not verdicts |
| 2 | Knowledgeable Peer | Has expertise but doesn't assert hierarchy, hedges occasionally |
| 3 | Informed Guide | Positions as ahead of the reader but not above them |
| 4 | Expert | Definitive statements, data-backed, minimal hedging |
| 5 | Absolute Authority | Commanding, categorical, assumes its perspective is the standard |

### Dimension 6 — Technicality

| Score | Label | Behavioral Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Plain Language | No jargon. Every term earns its place. Assumes no prior knowledge. |
| 2 | Accessible | Light technical vocabulary, always contextualized |
| 3 | Informed | Industry vocabulary used but defined on first use |
| 4 | Technical | Jargon standard, assumes domain familiarity |
| 5 | Specialist | Deep technical register, assumes expert-level reader |

### Dimension 7 — Directness

| Score | Label | Behavioral Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Indirect | Hedged, diplomatic, soft leads, multiple qualifications |
| 2 | Measured | Gets to the point but takes care with framing |
| 3 | Clear | Active voice standard, minimal hedging |
| 4 | Direct | States the point first, backs up after, blunt when warranted |
| 5 | Blunt | No softening. Active voice always. Cuts everything non-essential. |

## 4. "We Are X But Not Y" Mapping Table

Defines the brand's voice through boundary pairs. Each pair names what the brand IS and what it is NOT — then describes what that means in practice. These pairs prevent drift and resolve edge cases.

- We are **confident** but not **arrogant** — we state our expertise without implying others are inferior; we share proof, not posture.
- We are **warm** but not **saccharine** — we acknowledge emotion and show care, but we don't infantilize the reader or use hollow affirmations.
- We are **direct** but not **blunt** — we get to the point fast and use active voice, but we don't strip out the human context that makes directness feel respectful.
- We are **expert** but not **condescending** — we share knowledge from the front of the room, not from above the audience; we never imply the reader should already know this.
- We are **casual** but not **sloppy** — contractions and colloquialisms are fine; missing commas, vague antecedents, and lazy phrasing are not.
- We are **witty** but not **sarcastic** — we find humor in ideas and situations, not in mocking people or making readers feel on the outside of the joke.
- We are **bold** but not **reckless** — we make strong claims and take creative risks, but everything we say we can stand behind.
- We are **humble** but not **self-deprecating** — we acknowledge limitations honestly; we don't undermine our own credibility through excessive hedging or apology.
- We are **passionate** but not **preachy** — we believe deeply in what we do; we share that belief through demonstration, not through lecturing the audience.
- We are **approachable** but not **dumbed-down** — we write for a smart reader who is busy, not for a reader who cannot handle complexity.
- We are **simple** but not **simplistic** — we strip out unnecessary complexity; we don't strip out necessary nuance.
- We are **premium** but not **elitist** — our quality and craft signal high standards; our tone never signals exclusivity or signals that some readers don't belong.
- We are **innovative** but not **gimmicky** — we use fresh language and unexpected angles; we don't invent novelty for its own sake or chase trends that don't fit us.
- We are **honest** but not **brutal** — we tell the truth including uncomfortable truths; we frame them in ways that serve the reader, not just unburden the writer.
- We are **playful** but not **childish** — we let lightness in when it fits; we don't use playfulness as a cover for imprecision or a substitute for substance.

## 5. Voice vs Tone

### The Party Analogy

You have one voice. Your tone changes constantly. At a job interview you're still you — but you speak differently than with your best friend or at a funeral. Anyone who knows you would recognize you in all three contexts. That recognizable core is voice. The modulation is tone.

Voice is brand DNA. Tone is applied per context. Confusing them produces either robotic copy (identical everywhere) or incoherent copy (sounds like different companies wrote each channel).

### Voice vs Tone Table

| Dimension | Voice | Tone |
|---|---|---|
| What it is | Constant personality | Adapts to context and reader state |
| How often it changes | Never (unless full rebrand) | Every piece, every channel |
| Who owns it | Brand strategy | Writers, channel leads |
| Example | "Direct, warm, expert" | "More casual on social, more precise in docs" |
| Analogy | Your accent | Your volume and mood in the room |

### Tone Matrix by Context

| Context | Humor Level | Formality | Warmth | Directness |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Marketing / homepage | Moderate | Casual-neutral | High | High |
| Error message | Low-moderate | Casual | High | High |
| Support / help docs | Low | Neutral | High | High |
| Social media (organic) | High | Very casual | High | Moderate |
| Legal / terms | None | Formal | Low | High |
| Onboarding / welcome | Moderate | Casual | Very high | Moderate |

## 6. Card Sort (Margot Bloomstein Adapted)

A brand vocabulary exercise. Present adjectives as cards (physical or digital). Sort into: **Who we are** / **Who we are not** / **Don't care**. The "who we are not" pile is often more revealing. Run with 3–5 stakeholders independently — divergences signal unresolved identity disagreement.

### Adjective Card Bank

**Authority family**
- authoritative, knowledgeable, expert, trustworthy, credible

**Warmth family**
- friendly, approachable, caring, empathetic, supportive

**Energy family**
- bold, energetic, enthusiastic, passionate, dynamic

**Humor family**
- witty, playful, irreverent, fun, lighthearted

**Precision family**
- precise, clear, concise, straightforward, no-nonsense

**Premium family**
- elegant, refined, sophisticated, exclusive, premium

**Additional cards**
- inspiring, thought-provoking, rebellious, grounded, pragmatic, curious, candid, reassuring, aspirational, human

**Sort instructions:** Sort independently first. Limit "Who we are" to 8 cards max. Compare across stakeholders, note conflicts. Use the agreed set as the foundation for voice dimension targets.

## 8. Voice Discovery Questions Bank

Use in brand workshops, client interviews, or voice audits. Select 8–12 based on how much the brand already knows about itself — don't run the full set.

### Personality and Identity

1. If your brand were a person at a party, who are they? What are they wearing, who do they talk to, what's the first thing they say?
2. Name a celebrity, fictional character, or public figure whose voice feels close to what you want. What specifically about them fits?
3. If a stranger met your brand at that party and described them to a friend the next morning, what three adjectives would they use?
4. What three adjectives would you never want that stranger to use?
5. What would your brand say if it had to give a toast at a wedding — and what would it absolutely not say?

### Values and Differentiation

6. What do you believe that most of your competitors don't act on?
7. If you had to write the first line of a brand manifesto — one sentence — what would it be?
8. What feeling do you want someone to have 10 seconds after reading something from you?
9. What is the biggest misconception your industry has that you want to correct?

### Audience Relationship

10. What is your relationship to your audience — teacher, friend, expert, peer, coach? Why that one?
11. What does your audience already know when they first encounter you? What do they not know yet?
12. What emotional state are they usually in when they find you for the first time?
13. Are they looking for reassurance, information, motivation, or permission? What's your answer?

### Competitive Positioning

14. Pick three competitors. Describe their voice in two words each. Which words are completely off-limits for you because of those associations?
15. Whose voice do you most admire outside your category? What could you borrow from them?
16. What voice choices in your category make you cringe — and why?

### Practical Boundaries

17. Are there topics or contexts where humor becomes inappropriate for this brand? Name them specifically.
18. Is slang acceptable? Contractions? Sentence fragments? Where's the line?
19. What's the brand's policy on exclamation marks? On emoji?
20. How does this brand talk about competitors — directly, indirectly, or not at all?
21. If the brand makes a mistake, how does it apologize? What does the apology sound like?

### Forced-Choice Questions

22. When something is complex, does your brand simplify it for the reader or respect the complexity and explain it fully?
23. Short punchy sentences or longer developed ones? Which feels more like you?
24. One emoji per post or zero? What would prompt you to cross the line?
25. If the brand got something wrong publicly, does it lead with the apology or the fix?

## 9. Voice Testing Methods

Verify that guidelines work in practice, not just as documents.

### Method 1 — Competitor Swap Test

Replace your brand name with a competitor's in three pieces. If the copy still works for them, the voice isn't distinctive. Anything a competitor could publish unchanged is generic.

### Method 2 — Stranger Test

Show three unbranded pieces to someone outside the company. Ask: "Do these come from the same company?" If the answer is no or uncertain, voice consistency is failing in execution.

### Method 3 — "Would We Say This?" Read-Aloud Test

Read copy aloud as if the brand persona were a real person. Generic corporate filler sounds wrong immediately. Voice is an oral quality — reading silently hides what reading aloud surfaces.

### Method 4 — "But Not" Boundary Check

Run each piece against the "We Are X But Not Y" pairs from Section 4. Flag any line that crosses from intended characteristic into its opposite. Most useful for catching drift in high-volume production.

### Method 5 — Cocktail Party Test

Would the brand persona actually say this line at a party — in this register, at this energy level? If not, don't publish it. Catches both overly stiff and overly loose copy.

## 10. Real-World Voice Examples

Reference cases for what strong, consistent brand voice looks like in practice.

### Mailchimp

Fun but not silly. Confident but not cocky. Informal but not sloppy. Mailchimp pioneered context-dependent tone — their guide instructs writers to dial warmth and humor based on whether the user is celebrating a first campaign or troubleshooting a failed send. Situational awareness built into guidelines, not just adjective lists.

### Stripe

Technical precision meets accessibility. Documentation reads like an engineer who also writes well — no fluff, no condescension. Trusts the reader to be smart and rewards that trust with efficiency.

### Apple

Minimal, aspirational, human-centered. Every word earns its place. Short sentences are default; fragments used deliberately for emphasis. Never names competitors, never over-explains. Confidence expressed through what it omits.

### Innocent Drinks

Chatty, self-deprecating, relentlessly informal. Jester archetype executed with precision. Humor applied even to legal text — the medium's constraints never stop the voice. So consistent it has become a brand asset independent of the product.

### Slack

Friendly, clear, slightly witty. Sounds like your sharpest colleague — genuinely kind, explains things well, makes a dry observation that lands. Humor as punctuation, not the main event.

## 11. Voice Guide Validation Exercises

Run after drafting a voice guide to verify it works before wider rollout.

### Exercise 1 — Rewrite Challenge

Pull five off-brand pieces. Give them plus the voice guide to three writers independently. If rewrites diverge significantly, the guide isn't specific enough — fix the guidelines, not the writers.

### Exercise 2 — Voice Audit with Radar Chart

Pull ten random published pieces across three channels. Score each against the seven dimensions from Section 2. Plot a radar chart per piece. Radically different shapes = consistency failure. Clustered but offset from target = systematic drift.

### Exercise 3 — New Writer Test

Give the guide to someone new to the brand. Ask them to write three pieces across different contexts. If the output is recognizably on-brand without coaching, the guide works. If you correct multiple choices, those corrections reveal gaps in the guide.

### Exercise 4 — Context Flex Exercise

Write the same message (e.g., "we're changing our pricing") across four contexts: marketing email, support FAQ, social post, in-app notification. All four should feel like the same brand. If one version sounds like a different company, the tone matrix for that channel needs more definition.

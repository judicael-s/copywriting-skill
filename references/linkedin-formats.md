# LinkedIn Formats Reference

Comprehensive reference for LinkedIn copywriting: algorithm, formats, hooks, structure, CTAs, and common mistakes. Used by the social-copywriting skill.

---

## 1. Algorithm Factors

LinkedIn's feed algorithm rewards content that keeps people on the platform and generates meaningful interaction. Understanding the signal hierarchy is non-negotiable for effective copy.

### Signal Weight Table

| Signal | Weight | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Dwell time | Highest | Time spent reading/viewing — the most important factor |
| Comments (5+ words) | Very high | Short comments count less; substantive replies count most |
| Engagement velocity (first 60-90 min) | Very high | Early burst = algorithmic boost; post when your audience is online |
| Saves / bookmarks | High | Silent quality signal — people save what they find genuinely useful |
| Content relevance to viewer | High | LinkedIn infers topic relevance from your post history and profile |
| Native content | High | Content created on LinkedIn is prioritized over imported content |
| Expertise signals | Medium | Consistent topic focus builds topical authority over time |
| Reactions (other than comments) | Medium | Likes, celebrates, etc. — positive but weaker than comments |
| Shares / reposts | Medium | Distribution signal, but diluted across feed |
| Profile clicks | Medium | Indicates curiosity; signals post relevance |
| Link clicks | Low | Clicks happen but LinkedIn deprioritizes posts that send traffic away |
| Follows from a post | Medium | Strong intent signal when it happens |

### Penalization Triggers

| Behavior | Penalty | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| External links in post body | 30-50% reach reduction | Move links to first comment instead |
| Engagement bait | Filtered or suppressed | "Like if you agree," "Tag someone who needs this" |
| Hashtag stuffing (>5) | Reduced distribution | 3-5 relevant hashtags is the ceiling |
| Editing within the first hour | Reach reset | LinkedIn re-evaluates the post; early edits tank momentum |
| Rapid-fire posting (multiple per day) | Algorithmic dilution | One quality post per day maximum |
| Tagging non-engagers | Spam flag risk | Only tag people likely to respond meaningfully |
| Cross-posted content | Lower native score | Content clearly repurposed from other platforms underperforms |

## 3. Post Formats

### Text-Only Posts
- **Optimal length:** 1,200-1,600 characters (enough to develop an idea, short enough to respect attention)
- **Structure:** Hook → body → CTA. No images = hook must do more work.
- **Best for:** Opinion pieces, personal stories, quick insights, observations
- **When to use:** When the copy itself is the product. Text-only often outperforms image posts on thought leadership.
- **Formatting rule:** Single-line paragraphs separated by blank lines. No walls of text.

### Image Posts
- **Optimal dimensions:** 1200 × 1200 px (square) or 1200 × 627 px (landscape)
- **Caption strategy:** Caption does the heavy lifting — image stops the scroll, caption converts.
- **Hook placement:** First 2 lines of caption must hook before the "see more" fold.
- **Image copy:** Minimal text on image; 1 clear idea max.
- **Alt text:** Always write alt text (accessibility + signals to algorithm).

### Carousel / PDF Posts
- **Optimal slide count:** 8-12 slides (less feels thin; more loses people)
- **Title slide:** Must function as a hook — this is what appears in the feed. Treat it like a headline.
- **Copy per slide:** 20-40 words maximum. One idea per slide.
- **Last slide:** Always a CTA. Tell them what to do: follow, comment, save, DM.
- **Visual rule:** Consistent template, readable font, high contrast.
- **File type:** Upload as PDF. Native carousel is not available for personal posts.
- **Best for:** Frameworks, step-by-step guides, listicles, case studies

### Video Posts
- **Optimal length:** 30 seconds to 3 minutes (90 seconds is the sweet spot)
- **Captions:** Mandatory. 80%+ of LinkedIn video is watched without sound.
- **Upload:** Native upload only. YouTube/Vimeo links are penalized.
- **Hook:** First 3-5 seconds must justify continued watching.
- **Caption copy:** Write a full post caption, not just a title — same rules as text post.
- **Best for:** Demonstrations, behind-the-scenes, personal stories, event recaps

### Newsletter / Articles
- **Headline:** 40-49 characters performs best for click-through; 100 char limit
- **Body length:** 800-1,500 words for standard articles; newsletters can run longer
- **Structure:** Works best when structured like a blog post (H2s, short paragraphs, bullets)
- **Distribution:** Subscribers notified by push notification — higher intent readers
- **Best for:** Long-form insights, recurring series, depth on a single topic

### Polls
- **Question:** Specific and genuinely debatable — not "do you agree?" but "which approach works better for you?"
- **Options:** 2-4 options; max 30 characters each
- **Frequency:** 1-2 polls per month maximum; more than that feels lazy
- **Follow-up:** Always post a follow-up with the results — closes the loop and drives second-round engagement

## 4. Hook Formulas

The hook is the first 1-2 lines of the post — what appears before the "see more" fold. It determines whether anyone reads the rest.

### Formula 1: Contrarian
Challenge a widely held belief in the first line.

Structure: `[Common belief] is wrong.` or `Stop [common advice].`

Examples:
- "Posting every day on LinkedIn is killing your reach."
- "Your resume doesn't get you hired. This does."
- "More followers won't fix your lead problem."

### Formula 2: Story Open
Drop into a scene or moment. Tension must be implied or explicit immediately.

Structure: `I [unexpected event]. [Tension or stakes].`

Examples:
- "I got fired on a Tuesday. By Friday, I had 3 job offers."
- "I turned down a $200K offer last week. Here's why."
- "My best client fired me. It was the best thing that happened to my business."

### Formula 3: Data / Specific Number
Lead with a precise, surprising stat or count. Vague numbers kill credibility.

Structure: `[Specific number] + [unexpected or counterintuitive context]`

Examples:
- "I analyzed 500 LinkedIn posts. The top 1% all had this in common."
- "87% of hiring managers check your LinkedIn before your interview."
- "I spent $0 on ads and generated 47 leads last month. Here's the exact breakdown."

### Formula 4: Question
Open a knowledge gap the reader needs to close.

Structure: `[Question that reveals a gap the reader didn't know existed]`

Examples:
- "What's the one thing every viral LinkedIn post has that yours doesn't?"
- "When did you last look at your LinkedIn headline from a stranger's perspective?"
- "How many leads did your last post generate? If the answer is zero, read this."

### Formula 5: List / Number Promise
Promise a specific, countable set of things. Works best when the number is specific and the outcome is real.

Structure: `[Number] [things] that [specific outcome]`

Examples:
- "5 LinkedIn mistakes costing you clients right now"
- "The 3-step framework I use to write posts in 15 minutes"
- "7 phrases that kill trust in your profile headline"

### Formula 6: "I did X so you don't have to"
Position the post as a report from the field. Creates authority and saves the reader effort.

Examples:
- "I tested 12 posting times over 6 months. Here are the results."
- "I rewrote my headline 11 times before this version started converting."
- "I read every LinkedIn algorithm update from the past 2 years so you don't have to."

### Formula 7: Before / After
Show a transformation in one line. Numbers make it concrete.

Structure: `[Before state] → [After state]. Here's what changed.`

Examples:
- "6 months ago: 47 followers, 0 leads. Today: 22K followers, $15K/month."
- "My posts used to get 3 likes. This one got 4,200. The only thing I changed was the first line."
- "September: invisible on LinkedIn. March: 3 inbound deals from posts."

### Formula 8: Unpopular Opinion (use sparingly — overused on the platform)
State a real position you hold that most people would push back on. Must have genuine conviction behind it.

Structure: `Unpopular opinion: [actual position]`

Examples:
- "Unpopular opinion: Networking events are a waste of time for 90% of professionals."
- "Unpopular opinion: Your LinkedIn content is too polished. That's the problem."

Warning: This formula is saturated. Only use when you have a genuinely strong position to back up.

### Choosing a Hook Formula

No formula is universally best — match to your goal:
- **Highest engagement:** Story Open and Before/After (drive comments and shares through narrative)
- **Highest reach:** Contrarian and Myth-Busting (algorithm rewards debate and dwell time)
- **Safest for new accounts:** Direct Value and How-To (reliable performance, low risk)
- **Use sparingly:** Unpopular Opinion (saturated — audiences are fatigued on this format)

## 5. Post Structure Patterns

### AIDA
Best for: conversion-focused posts, offers, announcements

```
[A] Hook — grabs attention (1-2 lines)
[I] Interest — expand on the hook, build context (2-4 lines)
[D] Desire — show the benefit, outcome, or transformation (2-4 lines)
[A] Action — specific CTA (1 line)
```

### PAS
Best for: problem-aware audiences, pain-point-led copy

```
[P] Problem — name the exact problem clearly (1-2 lines)
[A] Agitate — make the reader feel why it matters / the cost of inaction (2-3 lines)
[S] Solution — introduce the solution clearly and specifically (2-4 lines)
+ CTA (1 line)
```

### BAB
Best for: transformation narratives, case studies, testimonials

```
[B] Before — describe the before state (2-3 lines)
[A] After — describe the after state, ideally with specifics (2-3 lines)
[B] Bridge — explain what caused the shift (3-5 lines)
+ CTA (1 line)
```

### 1-3-1
Best for: quick tips, listicle-style posts, takeaways

```
[1] One-line hook
[3] Three points (each 1-3 lines, separated by blank lines)
[1] One-line CTA or takeaway
```

## 6. Formatting Rules

- **Single-line paragraphs** separated by blank lines — no walls of text
- **Alternate paragraph lengths:** mix 1-line and 2-line paragraphs for visual rhythm
- **Emojis:** 0-3 total. Stick to functional ones: ✅ ➡️ 💡. Avoid decorative strings.
- **No Unicode bold or italic** (𝗯𝗼𝗹𝗱 characters) — often doesn't render, reads as spam
- **Bullets:** Use sparingly. Always wrap in a narrative — don't just bullet-dump without context.
- **Hashtags:** 3-5, placed at the end of the post. Mix 1 broad + 2-3 niche. No hashtags mid-text.
- **Line breaks:** Do not create artificial line breaks mid-sentence for drama — LinkedIn is not poetry Twitter.
- **No ALL CAPS** except for a single word for genuine emphasis (rarely).

## 7. CTA Patterns

Match the CTA to the goal. Mismatch between post content and CTA type = drop in conversion.

| CTA | Use Case | Spam Risk |
|---|---|---|
| "What would you add?" | Thought leadership, tips posts | Low |
| "Share your experience below." | Story posts, opinion posts | Low |
| "Repost if this resonated." | Broad-appeal insight posts | Medium |
| "Save this for later." | Reference content, frameworks, lists | Low |
| "Follow for more on [topic]." | Series content, niche expertise | Medium |
| "Link in the first comment." | External resource, guide, video | Low |
| "Tag someone who needs to see this." | Inspirational, relatable content | Medium-High |
| "Comment [X] and I'll send you [thing]." | Lead magnet delivery | High — penalized |
| "Like if you agree." | Any post | High — penalized |
| "DM me '[word]' for [resource]." | Soft lead gen | Medium |
| "I write about [topic] every [day]. Follow." | Profile growth | Medium |
| "Read the full breakdown — link in comments." | Deep-dive content | Low |

Avoid "Comment X and I'll send" and "Like if you agree" — LinkedIn's algorithm explicitly penalizes engagement bait.

## 8. Profile Copy

### Headline Formulas (220 char limit)

**Formula 1: Role + Who You Help + How**
`[Role] helping [audience] [achieve outcome] | [supporting credential]`
Example: `B2B Copywriter helping SaaS companies convert more trials to paid | $0 → $2M pipeline in 18 months`

**Formula 2: Outcome-Focused**
`[Specific outcome] for [audience] — [proof point or differentiator]`
Example: `More qualified leads from LinkedIn — without posting every day`

**Formula 3: Keyword Stack**
`[Job title] | [Specialization 1] | [Specialization 2] | [Specialization 3]`
Example: `Marketing Consultant | SEO | Paid Acquisition | CRO | B2B SaaS`

**Formula 4: Bold Claim + Proof**
`[Strong claim]. [Proof]. [What you do now]`
Example: `Built 3 content engines from 0. One hit 50M views. Now I teach the playbook.`

**Formula 5: Personality-Forward**
`[Who you are] + [what you believe] + [what you do]`
Example: `Marketer who hates marketing fluff. I write copy that converts, not impresses.`

### About Section Structure (2,600 char limit)

Paragraph 1 — Hook: One sentence that earns the second read. What's the most interesting thing about you?

Paragraph 2 — Story: What brought you here? Brief, honest, specific. No LinkedIn clichés ("passionate," "journey," "pivoting").

Paragraph 3 — What you do: Clear and specific. Who you work with, what problem you solve, how you solve it.

Paragraph 4 — Proof: Specific numbers, named outcomes, recognizable clients or contexts. Not adjectives.

Paragraph 5 — CTA: One clear next action. What should someone do if they're interested? DM you? Visit a page? No Calendly links in the first message.

### Featured Section Strategy
- Feature your single best post (most saves + comments)
- Feature one external link (article, portfolio, case study) — leads away so make it count
- Feature a video intro if you have one under 90 seconds
- Rotate every 60-90 days based on current priority

## 9. Content Pillars

### 5-Pillar Framework

| Pillar | Share | Content Type | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| Industry Insights | 30% | Trends, observations, analysis | Authority — makes you worth following |
| Behind-the-Scenes | 25% | Process, decisions, work-in-progress | Trust — makes you human |
| Educational | 25% | Frameworks, how-tos, breakdowns | Value — earns saves and shares |
| Personal | 15% | Stories, lessons, opinions | Likability — drives connection |
| Promotional | 5% | Offers, services, case studies | Revenue — don't skip it, don't overdo it |

### Know-Like-Trust Progression

New followers need Know content (educational, insight-driven) before they're ready for Personal or Promotional. Don't lead with self-promotion for cold audiences.

- **Know:** They find out you exist and understand your expertise
- **Like:** They connect with your perspective or story
- **Trust:** They believe your proof points and consider working with you

Calibrate pillar distribution to where your audience is in this progression.

### Series Content Strategy
- Commit to a recurring series (weekly or biweekly)
- Series title in the hook: "Growth breakdown #14:"
- Series content builds compounding authority — each post reinforces the last
- Best format for newsletters and carousels

## 10. Common Mistakes

**1. Hashtag abuse** — Using 10-15 hashtags looks desperate and dilutes relevance. Limit: 3-5.

**2. Engagement bait** — "Like if you agree" and "Tag someone who needs this" are flagged by the algorithm and damage reach.

**3. Corporate jargon** — "Leveraging synergies," "passionate about impact," "thought leadership." Sounds like a press release. Write like a person.

**4. The humble brag** — Framing every win as a lesson. "I'm so humbled by this award..." — pick a lane: celebrate or teach.

**5. Wall of text** — No line breaks, no white space, no mercy for the reader. Use paragraph breaks.

**6. No hook** — Starting with "I wanted to share..." or "Today I'd like to talk about..." — two lines of nothing.

**7. Over-posting** — Multiple posts per day dilutes authority and the algorithm punishes it. One quality post per day is the ceiling.

**8. Ignoring comments** — Comments in the first hour are critical for algorithmic distribution. Respond to every substantive comment within 60 minutes of posting.

**9. Too promotional** — More than 10% promotional content trains your audience to skip your posts. Earn attention before asking for anything.

**10. Copying viral formats without substance** — Posting a carousel "framework" with no real insight because it's the trending format. Format follows substance, not the other way around.

**11. Cross-posting without adaptation** — Pasting your Instagram caption or newsletter intro verbatim. Each platform has a different register, format, and reader expectation.
